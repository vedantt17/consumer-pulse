Macro Overview — How does Consumer Confidence compare to Actual Spending?

The Sentiment Index measures how optimistic Americans feel about the economy (0–100 scale; higher = more confident). PCE tracks what they actually spend each month. When these two diverge, it often signals a turning point in the economy.

Key Takeaway

Sentiment and spending usually move together — but divergences are warning signals. When confidence falls sharply (2008, 2020, 2022) spending tends to follow weeks to months later. Today’s below-average sentiment reading of 56.6 suggests potential spending softness ahead.

All series are converted to Z-scores so different categories can be compared fairly. A Z-score of 0 = exactly average; +2 = unusually high; −2 = unusually low.

Key Takeaway

Durable goods are the most sentiment-sensitive — people delay big-ticket purchases when confidence is low. Services (rent, insurance, subscriptions) are far more stable because many are recurring obligations. Non-durables (everyday necessities) barely budge even during crises.
